How Often Should You Clean Your Commercial Kitchen Hood?

The short answer: it depends on what you cook and how much of it you're cooking. The long answer is a bit more nuanced, and getting it wrong can cost you in fines, insurance headaches, or worse.

Here's how to figure out the right cleaning schedule for your kitchen.

The NFPA 96 Cleaning Schedule

The National Fire Protection Association sets the standard that fire marshals and insurance companies go by. Their recommended cleaning frequencies break down by cooking volume and type:

Monthly is the standard for kitchens using solid fuel cooking. If you're running a wood-fired oven, charcoal grill, or smoker, the combustion process creates heavier, stickier buildup in the exhaust system. Monthly cleaning is non-negotiable for these setups.

Every 3 months is the schedule for high-volume kitchens. This includes 24-hour restaurants, fast food operations, places doing heavy charbroiling, and any kitchen using wok cooking on a regular basis. The volume of grease-laden vapor these operations produce means buildup happens fast.

Every 6 months covers the majority of commercial kitchens. If you're running a typical sit-down restaurant, hotel kitchen, or standard commercial kitchen with moderate cooking volume, semi-annual cleaning is your baseline.

Once a year works for low-volume operations. Think church kitchens, seasonal cafes, summer camp facilities, and senior center kitchens that only cook a few times a week.

These Are Minimums, Not Maximums

A lot of restaurant owners treat the NFPA schedule as a target and schedule cleaning right at the deadline. That's technically compliant, but it's not always smart.

If your kitchen output increases (say you add a late-night menu, or your catering volume picks up), your grease production goes up too. The schedule that worked when you were doing 200 covers a night might not cut it at 400.

The practical test is simple: look at your hood filters and the visible interior of the hood canopy. If there's visible grease accumulation, it doesn't matter what the calendar says. It's time to clean.

What Counts as a "Cleaning"?

There's an important distinction between the daily maintenance your staff should be doing and the professional deep cleaning that NFPA 96 requires.

Daily maintenance (your staff) includes wiping down the exterior hood surfaces, removing and washing grease filters in hot soapy water or running them through the dishwasher, emptying grease cups and troughs, and making sure nothing is blocking airflow.

Professional cleaning (certified service) is the deep cleaning that hits every part of the system. This means the hood interior cleaned to bare metal, all ductwork scraped and degreased, the rooftop exhaust fan disassembled and cleaned, access panels opened and interior duct surfaces cleaned, and a full inspection of the fire suppression system components.

Your staff doing a great job on daily maintenance doesn't replace the professional cleaning. It just means the professional cleaning might go faster and you'll get better results.

Signs Your Kitchen Needs Cleaning Now

Between scheduled cleanings, keep an eye out for these warning signs that your system needs attention sooner than planned:

Grease is dripping from the hood or filters onto cooking surfaces or food. The exhaust fan seems louder or weaker than usual. There's a persistent grease smell in the kitchen even when nothing is cooking. Smoke isn't clearing the kitchen as quickly as it used to. You can see grease buildup on the inside of the hood canopy. Your grease cups are filling up faster than normal.

Any of these means grease is accumulating faster than your current schedule accounts for. Call your cleaning company and move up the appointment.

What About the Filters?

Hood filters are the first line of defense. They catch grease before it enters the ductwork. Clean filters mean less buildup in the ducts, which means your professional cleanings are more effective and your system works better between services.

Most kitchens should be cleaning filters daily or at minimum weekly. The exact frequency depends on your cooking volume. A busy burger joint might need to pull and wash filters every shift. A bakery that mostly uses ovens might get away with weekly.

The key is that filters should never have visible grease dripping off them. If they do, they're overdue.

How to Stay on Schedule

The easiest approach is to set up a recurring service with a professional hood cleaning company. Most companies will put you on a schedule and send reminders when your next cleaning is coming up. They'll also track your service history, which makes it easy to pull records when an inspector asks.

Keep a log in your kitchen (digital or paper) that tracks every professional cleaning date, the company that did it, and the next scheduled service. Your fire inspector will want to see this, and it takes about 30 seconds to maintain.
