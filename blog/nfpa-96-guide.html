NFPA 96 Compliance Guide: What Every Restaurant Owner Needs to Know

If you own or manage a commercial kitchen, NFPA 96 is the set of rules you need to follow to stay open, stay insured, and stay safe. It's the national standard for fire prevention in commercial cooking operations, and it covers everything from how your exhaust hood is installed to how often it gets cleaned.

This guide breaks down what NFPA 96 actually requires in plain language so you're not guessing.

What Is NFPA 96?

NFPA stands for the National Fire Protection Association. They publish codes and standards that local fire marshals, health inspectors, and insurance companies use to determine whether your kitchen is safe to operate.

NFPA 96 specifically covers ventilation control and fire protection for commercial cooking operations. That includes your exhaust hood, ductwork, rooftop fan, grease filters, fire suppression system, and the cooking equipment underneath all of it.

Think of it this way: if grease vapor touches it, NFPA 96 has something to say about it.

Who Needs to Follow NFPA 96?

Any commercial kitchen that produces grease-laden vapors during cooking. That covers restaurants, food trucks, hotel kitchens, hospital cafeterias, school kitchens, catering operations, ghost kitchens, and pretty much any place where food is cooked for the public on a commercial scale.

If you have a Type 1 exhaust hood (the kind that captures grease), NFPA 96 applies to you.

How Often Does Your Hood System Need to Be Cleaned?

This is the part most people care about, and the answer depends on what kind of cooking you do and how much of it happens. NFPA 96 lays out a specific schedule:

Monthly cleaning is required for systems connected to solid fuel cooking equipment. That means wood-fired pizza ovens, charcoal grills, smokers, and similar setups. Solid fuel produces more combustible buildup than gas or electric, so the cleaning cycle is shorter.

Quarterly cleaning (every 3 months) applies to high-volume operations. If your kitchen runs 24 hours, does heavy charbroiling, or uses wok cooking regularly, you fall into this category. Fast food restaurants and high-output diners typically need quarterly service.

Semi-annual cleaning (every 6 months) is the most common schedule and covers moderate-volume kitchens. Most sit-down restaurants, hotel kitchens, and standard commercial kitchens land here.

Annual cleaning is for low-volume operations like church kitchens, seasonal businesses, day camps, and senior centers that cook infrequently.

These are minimums. If your kitchen produces a lot of grease, you may need cleaning more often regardless of what category you fall into. The general rule: if you can see grease buildup, it's time.

What Does a Proper Hood Cleaning Include?

A compliant cleaning isn't just wiping down the visible parts of the hood. NFPA 96 requires that the entire exhaust system be cleaned to bare metal. That means:

The hood canopy itself, including the interior surfaces, grease gutters, and filter tracks. The grease filters, which should be removed, degreased, and inspected. The ductwork running from the hood up through the building to the roof. The exhaust fan on the rooftop, including the fan blades, housing, and hinge kit. Any access panels or clean-out doors in the duct system.

If a cleaning company only wipes the visible canopy and swaps filters, that's not a compliant cleaning. The grease hiding in the ducts and around the fan is where fires actually start.

What Documentation Should You Get?

After every cleaning, your service provider should leave you with a few things. A certification sticker on the hood showing the date of cleaning and the next scheduled service. A written report detailing what was cleaned, what chemicals were used, and the condition of the system. Before-and-after photos of the cleaned areas are also standard practice with reputable companies.

Keep all of this in a binder or digital folder. Fire inspectors will ask to see it, and your insurance company may request it if you ever file a claim.

What Happens If You Don't Comply?

The consequences of ignoring NFPA 96 stack up quickly. Health department violations can result in fines, required re-inspections, or temporary closure. Your insurance company can deny a fire claim if you can't prove you were maintaining the system on schedule. And most seriously, grease fires in exhaust systems spread fast, move through ductwork, and can cause catastrophic damage to the entire building.

According to the NFPA, cooking equipment is the leading cause of restaurant fires, and failure to clean is the primary factor in fires that start in exhaust systems.

How to Choose a Hood Cleaning Company

Not every company that offers hood cleaning actually does it right. Here's what to look for:

Ask if they follow NFPA 96 standards specifically. This means they clean the entire system to bare metal, not just the easy-to-reach surfaces.

Check for certifications. Companies certified by IKECA (International Kitchen Exhaust Cleaning Association) have been trained and tested on proper cleaning procedures.

Make sure they carry insurance. General liability and workers' compensation are the minimum. If something goes wrong during the cleaning, you need to know you're covered.

Ask for before-and-after photos. Any company that does thorough work will document it. If they can't or won't provide photos, that's a red flag.

Get a written report after every service. This is your proof of compliance for inspectors and insurers.
